## Introduction

What should a buyer verify when sourcing BPA-free paper baking cups? Start with the complete finished construction, not the word “paper.” Identify the base paper, grease or release treatment, printing ink, coating, adhesive and any silicone or plastic layer; define the food, contact time and baking conditions; then match the supplier declaration and supporting evidence to that exact SKU. “BPA-free” addresses one substance claim. It does not by itself prove overall food-contact compliance, oven performance or suitability for an EU and US program.

The distinction is especially important in the EU. The European Commission’s official [implementation guidance for Regulation (EU) 2024/3190](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/PDF/?uri=CELEX%3A52025XC06721) says paper and board are not themselves among the material groups listed in the Regulation. However, a paper article may include printing inks, adhesives, silicones, varnishes or coatings that are in scope. A finished printed or treated baking cup can therefore require a declaration of compliance under the Regulation even though the paper substrate alone is not listed.

The United States uses a different framework. FDA says currently approved BPA uses in food containers and packaging remain safe, while the regulatory status of a finished food-contact material depends on its individual substances, intended use and applicable limitations. A buyer should not treat an EU declaration as an automatic US clearance, or an FDA reference as proof of conformity with the EU restriction.

This guide turns those differences into a practical purchasing workflow for samples, application testing, document review, packaging, inspection and change control.

## 1. Define What “BPA-Free” Means for the Purchased SKU

A purchase specification should state the claim, product and evidence boundary. “BPA-free paper” may refer only to the base sheet. “BPA-free coating” may say nothing about an adhesive or printing ink. “BPA-free finished baking cup” is broader, but still needs a defined analytical or supply-chain basis and must not be mistaken for a complete safety conclusion.

Build a construction sheet for the exact item:

- product format, such as pleated liner, tulip cup, self-supporting cup or roll-mouth cup;
- paper grade, colour, grammage and supplier or internal material code;
- grease barrier, release treatment, coating or lamination;
- printing process, ink system, coverage and print-side orientation;
- adhesive or forming aid, if used;
- any silicone, plastic, varnish or other functional component;
- finished dimensions, pack count, artwork revision and destination markets;
- intended food, maximum contact time, baking temperature and other foreseeable use.

Ask the supplier whether BPA or another relevant bisphenol is intentionally used in any in-scope component, what upstream statements support that answer, and whether testing applies to raw material or the finished article. Record the exact wording. “Not intentionally added,” “not used in manufacture,” “not detected above a stated method limit” and “complies with Regulation (EU) 2024/3190” are different statements.

Do not silently convert one into another. A non-detect result depends on the sampled item, method, extraction, detection limit and laboratory report. A formulation declaration relies on controlled composition and supply-chain information. Both can be useful when their scope is clear.

## 2. Apply the EU Rule to Components, Not Just Paper

The current [consolidated text of Regulation (EU) 2024/3190](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32024R3190) prohibits the use of BPA and its salts in specified food-contact material groups, subject to limited authorised applications. Its listed groups include adhesives, rubbers, ion-exchange resins, plastics, printing inks, silicones, and varnishes and coatings. It also controls certain hazardous bisphenols and derivatives.

This produces a practical component test for paper baking cups:

| Part of the finished cup | Buyer question | Useful evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Base paper or board | Is this only untreated paper, or does the supplier’s statement include additives and surface treatment? | Material specification, supplier identity, grade code and change history |
| Printing ink | Is the ink food-contact relevant, on which side is it printed, and was BPA or another restricted bisphenol used in its manufacture? | Ink supplier declaration, print specification, GMP controls and exact colour-system scope |
| Adhesive | Is adhesive present in the finished construction or retail pack, and is its food-contact role defined? | Formulation or compliance statement tied to the adhesive code and intended use |
| Silicone, coating or varnish | What chemistry creates release or barrier performance, and is it in scope? | Component declaration, technical specification and supporting test or assessment |
| Complete formed cup | Do all declarations match the production SKU, artwork and use conditions? | Finished-article declaration, representative report, bill of materials and sample code |

Article 8 requires covered materials and articles not yet in contact with food to be accompanied at non-retail marketing stages by a written declaration of compliance, with supporting documentation available to authorities. Buyers should review the information required by Annex III and confirm that the declaration identifies the issuing business, material or article, date, relevant bisphenol use and compliance basis.

The official guidance answers a key paper-buyer question directly: paper and board alone are outside the listed material groups, but combining them with an in-scope material can bring the resulting article within the Regulation’s declaration requirement. That is more precise than asking for a generic “EU certificate.”

## 3. Check the 2026 EU Transition Against the Real Supply Chain

Regulation 2024/3190 entered into force in January 2025 and was later corrected by [Commission Regulation (EU) 2026/250](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2026/250/oj/eng). The consolidated provisions state that single-use final food-contact articles manufactured using BPA under the earlier rules may generally be placed on the market until 20 July 2026, with limited longer periods for specified applications that are not typical paper baking cups.

The date should trigger a file review, not a slogan. Confirm:

- whether the product is a final single-use food-contact article;
- whether any in-scope component was manufactured using BPA;
- when the specific goods were manufactured and first placed on the EU market;
- whether a stated transition actually applies to that SKU;
- which entity holds the declaration and supporting documentation;
- whether later corrections, guidance or enforcement information affect the decision.

An importer planning deliveries around July 2026 should use the current consolidated text and review the facts for each SKU. “Old stock is allowed” is not enough without product identity, manufacture records, dates and legal basis.

## 4. Keep the US Review Separate

FDA’s [BPA questions and answers](https://www.fda.gov/food/food-packaging-other-substances-come-contact-food-information-consumers/questions-answers-bisphenol-bpa-use-food-contact-applications) state that available information continues to support the safety of BPA for currently approved uses in food containers and packaging. FDA also explains that its evaluation considers migration, the nature of packaging components and safe exposure levels. This is not the same legal position as the EU prohibition.

For a US paper baking cup, use FDA’s [component regulatory-status framework](https://www.fda.gov/food/packaging-food-contact-substances-fcs/determining-regulatory-status-components-food-contact-material). It says the overall status of a food-contact material is dictated by each substance that comprises the article and may reasonably be expected to migrate. The basis may be an applicable regulation, GRAS status, prior sanction, a threshold-of-regulation exemption or an effective Food Contact Substance Notification. An FCN is effective for the identified manufacturer, substance and conditions of use; it is not automatically transferable to a similar substance from another source.

Ask for the US basis component by component, including identity, specifications and limitations. Then define the application using FDA’s [food types and conditions of use](https://www.fda.gov/food/packaging-food-contact-substances-fcs/food-types-conditions-use-food-contact-substances). A liner used with fatty batter under oven heat is not represented by evidence for a dry food at room temperature.

If the US brief requires a “BPA-free” claim, review it separately from legal food-contact status. Confirm its scope and evidence, and do not present an EU rule as an FDA requirement.

## 5. Build a Document Review Matrix

The strongest procurement file connects claims to a controlled product identity. Use a matrix rather than collecting unrelated PDF certificates.

| Record | What it should identify | Common gap |
|---|---|---|
| Finished-product specification | SKU, dimensions, materials, print, treatment, pack and intended use | Catalogue name with no material codes |
| Bill of materials | Paper, ink, adhesive, coating, silicone and other components | “Food-grade paper” used as the whole construction |
| EU declaration of compliance | Issuer, article, date, applicable rule and information required by Annex III | Declaration for a raw sheet rather than the printed formed cup |
| Supporting report | Sample identity, method, detection limit, result and laboratory details | Result cannot be linked to the current SKU or revision |
| US regulatory statement | Component authorization basis and conditions of use | Generic “FDA approved” wording without a citation or supplier match |
| GMP and change-control record | Controlled formulation, print orientation, set-off controls and revision approval | No trigger for a new ink, coating or source |
| Traceability record | Production lot, material lots, pack code and shipment | Documents cannot be connected to cartons inspected |

EU [Regulation (EC) No 1935/2004](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32004R1935) remains the framework requirement: food-contact materials must not transfer constituents in quantities that endanger health, unacceptably change food composition or deteriorate organoleptic characteristics under normal or foreseeable use. It also requires traceability.

[Regulation (EC) No 2023/2006](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32006R2023) adds good manufacturing practice. For printing, buyers should confirm controls against transfer through the substrate and set-off from printed surfaces in stacks or reels. A BPA statement does not replace GMP, and GMP does not prove a substance-specific test result.

For a deeper report-scope review, use the [food-contact testing guide for paper baking cups](/resources/food-contact-testing-paper-baking-cups/). It shows how to match product identity, material construction, food type, time, temperature and destination-market basis.

## 6. Approve a Representative Sample Through the Real Application

Chemical documentation does not show whether a cup performs in the buyer’s bakery, pan, oven and distribution process. Approve a sample made with the intended paper, treatment, print, tooling and pack configuration. Record a sample code and photograph it beside the specification revision.

Run a controlled trial that includes:

1. Measure base diameter, top opening, wall height and nesting against the approved drawing.
2. Check dry odour, visible contamination, print rub and set-off before filling.
3. Fill with the actual batter at the planned portion weight, including a high-fat or high-moisture recipe when relevant.
4. Bake at the intended temperature and time in the production pan or support system.
5. Record grease staining, release, wall deformation, colour change, print condition and abnormal odour after cooling.
6. Handle, decorate, wrap and transport the finished food as the customer will.
7. Retain approved and tested samples with the report, lot and material revision.

For custom print, the [cupcake-liner artwork approval checklist](/resources/custom-cupcake-liner-artwork-checklist/) helps control dielines, colour references, ink coverage, print-side orientation and file revision before the compliance sample is made.

## 7. Control Packaging, Storage and Shipment Identity

Paper baking cups can deform, absorb odour or pick up dust if inner packs and cartons are poorly specified. The packaging plan should preserve the tested article and make the compliance file traceable to the shipment.

Specify clean inner packaging, pack count, bag or sleeve closure, carton strength, carton quantity, dividers where needed, lot marking and pallet protection. Test representative cartons for the expected distribution route. A compressed or moisture-affected cup may fail at the bakery even when its material declarations remain valid.

Pre-shipment inspection should verify:

- SKU, artwork revision, product and carton lot codes;
- dimensions, colour, forming, nesting and workmanship;
- dry odour, visible contamination, print rub and set-off;
- inner-pack integrity, count and label accuracy;
- master-carton marks, quantity and pallet configuration;
- document revision and correspondence to the inspected lot;
- any approved deviations and their disposition.

Set sampling, defect classes and acceptance criteria in the contract according to product and buyer risk. Do not use an unexplained universal AQL. The [baking paper quality-control guide](/resources/baking-paper-products-quality-control-inspection/) provides a practical structure for lot selection, measurements, defect classification and shipment records.

## 8. Add Change Control to the Purchase Order

A compliant sample can become irrelevant if a supplier changes paper mill, ink, adhesive, coating, release treatment or forming process without review. The purchase order should require advance notification and buyer approval for changes that affect construction, food-contact evidence, appearance or performance.

Define reapproval triggers:

- new component manufacturer or material code;
- formulation or treatment change;
- different printing process, ink series or coverage;
- new production site, tooling or forming method;
- changed intended use, food type, time or temperature;
- revised artwork or private-label claim;
- new destination market or applicable rule;
- test method, declaration or report expiry or supersession.

After a trigger, decide which documents and tests must be repeated. A new coating, ink or supplier may require a revised declaration, supporting evidence and application trial. Keep the decision with the SKU history.

## 9. Include EUDR Traceability Without Calling It a Certificate

BPA and food-contact controls address chemical and use-related questions. EUDR addresses deforestation due diligence for commodities and products within scope. They are separate workstreams.

The European Commission’s current [EUDR overview](https://environment.ec.europa.eu/topics/forests/deforestation/regulation-deforestation-free-products_en) lists application from 30 December 2026 for large and medium operators and 30 June 2027 for micro and small operators, while micro and small operators already covered by the former EU Timber Regulation follow the stated 30 December 2026 date. Buyers should confirm the relevant product code, role and current legal text.

For applicable paper products, request available paper-origin, species, country, geolocation, supplier and batch records early enough for the EU operator’s due diligence. EUDR is not a product certificate, and an FSC document does not automatically complete EUDR duties. Use the [EUDR paper baking cup traceability checklist](/resources/eudr-paper-baking-cups-traceability-checklist/) to separate scope assessment, evidence collection and operator responsibilities from BPA documentation.

- food type, batter characteristics, bake temperature, time and pan;

Compare quotations only after suppliers answer the same specification. Record excluded testing, packing, controlled materials, traceability and unresolved evidence beside the unit price.

## FAQ

### Are paper baking cups automatically BPA-free?

No. Paper itself does not establish the composition of a finished cup. Printing inks, adhesives, coatings, silicones and other treatments may be relevant. Ask for a construction-specific statement and supporting evidence tied to the exact SKU.

### Does EU Regulation 2024/3190 apply to paper and board?

The Commission’s implementation guidance says paper and board are not themselves listed in the Regulation’s material groups. However, a paper article combined with an in-scope printing ink, adhesive, coating, silicone or other listed material can be covered, including the declaration requirement.

### Is a BPA-free claim the same as EU food-contact compliance?

No. It addresses a limited substance claim. EU food-contact review also considers the complete construction, normal and foreseeable use, migration, organoleptic effects, GMP, traceability and any applicable national or specific measures.

### Does an FDA statement prove that a cup is BPA-free?

Not by itself. US regulatory status depends on each relevant substance, its authorization basis and conditions of use. A BPA-free commercial claim requires its own defined scope and evidence. Avoid generic “FDA approved” language without a specific basis.

### Should buyers test the raw paper or the finished printed cup?

Upstream paper and component evidence is useful, but the finished printed and formed article is the closest representation of the product being purchased. The testing plan should be risk-based and matched to construction, intended use and market requirements.

### What should trigger reapproval of a BPA-free baking cup?

A new paper, ink, adhesive, coating, silicone, supplier, production site, artwork, intended use or destination market can change the evidence needed. Define notification and reapproval rules in the specification and purchase order.

## Conclusion

Sourcing BPA-free paper baking cups is a component-control and evidence-matching task. Start with the complete finished construction, separate EU and US legal bases, and define exactly what the BPA-free claim covers. In the EU, bare paper and board are not listed in Regulation 2024/3190, but in-scope inks, adhesives, coatings, silicones or other components can bring a finished paper article within its requirements. In the United States, evaluate each component against its authorization and conditions of use.

The most defensible buying file links the specification, bill of materials, declarations, reports, representative samples, bake trials, packaging, lot inspection and change history. That process gives procurement, quality and compliance teams evidence they can actually review instead of relying on a logo or generic certificate name.
